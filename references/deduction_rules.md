# Deduction Rules & CPA Flags — Canadian Tax (2024/2025)

## Home Office Deduction

### Who qualifies?
- Self-employed: workspace must be principal place of business OR used exclusively and regularly to meet clients
- Employed (T4): employer must sign T2200 (Declaration of Conditions of Employment); work-from-home counts

### Two Methods

**Simplified Method (CRA Flat Rate)**
- $2 per day worked from home (max 200 days = $400/year)
- No receipts required; no T2200 needed for employees
- Available for 2020–2022 COVID years; still valid for self-employed via T2125

**Detailed Method (T777 / T2125)**
- Calculate: (office sq ft ÷ total sq ft) × eligible home expenses
- Eligible expenses: rent OR mortgage interest (NOT principal), property tax, home insurance, heat/utilities, internet (business %)
- Capital expenses (renovations) NOT deductible through home office; may be added to ACB
- Home office deduction cannot create or increase a business loss (carry forward allowed)

### ⚠️ Common Home Office Pitfalls
- Cannot claim both simplified and detailed in the same year
- Do not double-count: if internet is claimed under home office, don't also claim it under business expenses
- Mortgage PRINCIPAL is never deductible — only interest
- CRA may ask to see a floor plan with dimensions

## Vehicle Expenses

### Who qualifies?
Must use vehicle for earning business income. Personal-use portion is NOT deductible.

### Required records
- **Mileage log** (mandatory): Date, destination, business purpose, km driven
- Calculate: (business km ÷ total km) = business-use percentage
- Applies to: gas, insurance, repairs, maintenance, registration, depreciation (CCA)

### CCA for vehicles (Class 10.1)
- Personal-use vehicles (cost > $37,000 in 2024): limited to CCA on $37,000 maximum
- Class 10: vehicles ≤ $37,000 — 30% declining balance
- Class 10.1: vehicles > $37,000 — 30% declining balance on capped cost
- Zero-emission vehicles: Class 54/55, up to $61,000

### ⚠️ Flags
- No mileage log = CRA will likely deny vehicle claim entirely
- Car wash, rims, accessories: minor; need to confirm business use
- Car insurance: only the business-use percentage is deductible
- Leased vehicles: deductible lease payments are capped ($1,100/month for 2024)

## Meals & Entertainment

- **50% rule**: Only 50% of meal/entertainment expenses are deductible
- Must be for business purposes and documented (who attended, business purpose)
- Long-haul truckers exception: 80% for meals away from home
- Client appreciation events over $100/person need detailed documentation
- **Tip:** Record the business purpose on the back of each receipt

---

## Gift Cards & Client Gifts

- **Generally NOT deductible** as a business expense
- Exception: promotional items bearing company name (pens, calendars) — 100% deductible if <$4 each
- **Client gifts:** Only deductible if they're a form of advertising or business promotion
- CRA does not have a specific "$300 per client" deduction — this is often confused with the employee gift rule
- CPA should review any gift card purchases > $200 to assess risk

---

## Software & Subscriptions

- **Fully deductible** if used for business (100% business use)
- Mixed use: claim only the business-use percentage
- Examples: accounting software, project management tools, AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude), cloud storage, design tools
- One-time software purchases > $500: may need to be capitalized under CCA Class 12 (100% in year of purchase) or Class 10

---

## Professional Development

- **Fully deductible** for self-employed: courses, certifications, books, conferences
- For T4 employees: not deductible unless employer requires it and signs T2200
- Must be related to existing profession/business — not for entering a new field

---

## Insurance

| Type | Deductibility |
|------|--------------|
| Business liability / E&O insurance | 100% deductible |
| Business vehicle insurance | Business-use % only |
| Home insurance (home office) | Office-use % only |
| Life insurance | Generally NOT deductible (unless corporate/key-man) |
| Personal health insurance premiums | May qualify as medical expense credit |

---

## Travel (Business)

- Flights, hotels, taxis for business trips: 100% deductible
- Must document business purpose for each trip
- Combined business + personal trip: only business-related portion deductible
- Meals during travel: still subject to 50% rule
- Travel for training/conferences: deductible if reasonable and business-related

---

## Banking & FX Fees

- Bank service charges, wire fees, FX conversion fees: deductible for business accounts
- Interest on business loans/credit cards: deductible (Line 8710)
- Interest on personal loans used for investment: deductible under Line 22100 (carrying charges)

## Capital Cost Allowance (CCA) Quick Reference

| Class | Assets | Rate |
|-------|--------|------|
| 8 | Office furniture, equipment | 20% declining balance |
| 10 | Vehicles ≤ $37K | 30% declining balance |
| 10.1 | Vehicles > $37K | 30% (capped cost) |
| 12 | Software, small tools < $500 | 100% (year of purchase) |
| 50 | Computers, servers | 55% |
| 54 | Zero-emission vehicles | 30%+ (higher rates) |

**Half-year rule:** In the year of acquisition, only 50% of the normal CCA rate can be claimed.

## Registered Accounts — Tax Treatment Summary

| Account | Contributions | Growth | Withdrawals |
|---------|--------------|--------|-------------|
| RRSP | Deductible | Tax-deferred | Fully taxable |
| TFSA | Not deductible | Tax-free | Tax-free |
| RESP | Not deductible | Tax-deferred | EAP taxable in child's hands; principal returned tax-free |
| Non-registered | After-tax | Taxable annually | Capital gains on disposition |

## Common CRA Red Flags (Items That Attract Audits)

1. Vehicle expenses > 90% business use (very rare; needs strong mileage log)
2. Home office expense >> other self-employment expenses
3. Meals & entertainment that seem disproportionate to business size
4. Large business losses several years in a row (questions whether it's a business or hobby)
5. Round numbers everywhere (suggests estimating rather than tracking)
6. Inconsistency between reported income and lifestyle (CRA uses third-party data)
7. Cash-heavy businesses with no paper trail

## Currency Conversion — USD to CAD

- Use Bank of Canada (BOC) noon rate on the date of the transaction
- For multiple similar transactions, a reasonable average rate for the year is acceptable
- For T5008 slips reported in USD: convert using BOC rate on trade date
- Always document the rate used in the workbook notes
- BOC historical rates: https://www.bankofcanada.ca/rates/exchange/legacy-noon-and-closing-rates/
